DINO-Foresight: Looking into the Future with DINO

Efstathios Karypidis  ·  Ioannis Kakogeorgiou  ·  Spyros Gidaris (valeo.ai)  ·  Nikos Komodakis
Archimedes / Athena Research Center  ·  National Technical University of Athens  ·  University of Crete
Semantic Future Prediction Vision Foundation Models World Model Autonomous Driving Self-supervised

Abstract

Predicting future dynamics is crucial for applications like autonomous driving and robotics, where understanding the environment is key. Existing pixel-level methods are computationally expensive and often focus on irrelevant details. To address these challenges, we introduce DINO-Foresight, a novel framework that operates in the semantic feature space of pretrained Vision Foundation Models (VFMs). Our approach trains a masked feature transformer in a self-supervised manner to predict the evolution of VFM features over time. By forecasting these features, we can apply off-the-shelf, task-specific heads for various scene understanding tasks. In this framework, VFM features are treated as a latent space, to which different heads attach to perform specific tasks for future-frame analysis. Extensive experiments show the very strong performance, robustness and scalability of our framework.

Problem and Motivation

Core Gap

Dominant video prediction methods (VISTA, MaskViT, latent diffusion models) generate future RGB frames by forecasting over VAE latent spaces. VAE latents encode low-level appearance information; they are poor targets for downstream semantic tasks and force the model to simultaneously model both texture and semantics. A DPT Oracle trained on VAE latents achieves only 47.3 mIoU versus 77.0 for DINOv2 features (Table 2), confirming the representation mismatch.

Pain Point 1

VAE latent spaces are not semantically aligned → task heads cannot attach without catastrophic performance loss.

Pain Point 2

Prior semantic feature prediction methods (F2F, PFA) require separate models per task, limiting scalability.

Pain Point 3

Diffusion-based world models (VISTA, 2.5B params) require 8.3 h for Cityscapes val inference vs 5 min for DINO-Foresight.

Key Insight

Frozen VFM features already encode semantic structure. Forecasting them directly avoids pixel synthesis and inherits VFM generalizability for free.

Module 1: Hierarchical Feature Construction

Multi-layer features from the frozen ViT (layers 3, 6, 9, 12) are concatenated and PCA-compressed to dimension D. This reduces modeling difficulty while retaining semantic content. PCA marginally improves over no reduction (Table 5).

Module 2: Masked Feature Transformer

Factorized temporal + spatial attention over N×H×W tokens. Context tokens are passed as-is; future tokens are [MASK] vectors. Trained with SmoothL1 loss (β=0.1) on masked positions. Full masking outperforms random masking (Table 9).

Module 3: Resolution Adaptation

Two-phase training: low-resolution (224×448) first, then fine-tuned at high-resolution (448×896). Outperforms sliding-window and position-embedding interpolation strategies (Table 4, best mIoU 71.81).

Module 4: Modular Task Heads

DPT for semantic segmentation, depth, surface normals; Mask2Former for instance segmentation. Heads are trained once on static VFM features and applied directly to predicted future features -- no retraining of core predictor.

Training Objective

$$\mathcal{L}_{MFM} = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \mathcal{L}\!\left(F_{TRG}(p),\, \tilde{F}_{TRG}(p)\right)\right]$$

SmoothL1 loss with β=0.1 applied to all H×W positions of the N_p future frames. P covers all future spatial positions.

Key Ablation Findings

VFM Encoder Choice

DINOv2 > EVA2-CLIP > SAM across all tasks. VAE latents are catastrophically worse: Oracle mIoU 47.3 vs DINOv2 77.0 (Table 2).

Masking Strategy

Full masking (all future tokens) consistently outperforms random masking across semantic seg, depth, and surface normals (Table 9).

Model Scale

Small (115M) → Base (258M) → Large (460M): monotonic improvement, especially mid-term. Framework scales predictably (Table 11).

Multi-Layer Features

Layers {3,6,9,12} vs layer {12} only: ALL/MO rises from 72.1/73.4 to 77.0/77.4 (Table 8).

Contributions Assessment

Supported

  • Single model for four dense prediction tasks where prior methods require multiple models -- Table 1 and Table 6 confirm.
  • Orders-of-magnitude inference speedup vs diffusion-based baseline (5 min vs 8.3 h on Cityscapes val, single A100).
  • Zero-shot cross-dataset generalization: Cityscapes-trained model applied to nuScenes with minimal degradation (Table 7).
  • Scalability: consistent improvements with model size (115M to 460M) and data diversity (Table 11, Table 12).

Limitations and Caveats

  • Model is deterministic; cannot represent future uncertainty or multimodality -- structurally insufficient for high-uncertainty driving scenarios.
  • Depth and surface normal evaluation relies on pseudo-GT, which introduces an upper-bound ceiling; absolute performance claims on these tasks require careful interpretation.
  • Asymmetric context length: DINO-Foresight uses 4 context frames, VISTA uses 3; comparison is not perfectly controlled.
  • PCA compression ratio D and layer selection are empirically fixed without principled justification.

Open Questions

How does performance degrade with autoregressive rollouts beyond the qualitative 48-frame experiment in Appendix A.7?

Would replacing SmoothL1 with a diffusion or flow-matching head substantially change results on high-motion scenes?

Are per-frame depth and normal predictions temporally consistent, or do they drift without explicit temporal smoothness constraints?

How sensitive is performance to PCA dimensionality D and the specific VFM layer selection?
