DriveWorld-VLA

Unified Latent-Space World Modeling with Vision-Language-Action for Autonomous Driving

arXiv 2602.06521  |  2026-02-06  |  Preprint  |  Beijing Jiaotong University & Xiaomi EV
VLA World Model Latent Planning BEV Generation Flow Matching E2E Autonomous Driving

Abstract

End-to-end (E2E) autonomous driving has recently attracted increasing interest in unifying Vision-Language-Action (VLA) with World Models to enhance decision-making and forward-looking imagination. However, existing methods fail to effectively unify future scene evolution and action planning within a single architecture due to inadequate sharing of latent states, limiting the impact of visual imagination on action decisions. DriveWorld-VLA unifies world modeling and planning within a latent space by tightly integrating VLA and world models at the representation level. It incorporates the latent states of the world model as core decision-making states for the VLA planner, enabling assessment of how candidate actions impact future scene evolution. World modeling is performed entirely in the latent space, supporting controllable, action-conditioned imagination at the feature level, avoiding expensive pixel-level rollouts.

Problem and Motivation

E2E autonomous driving has shifted toward integrating VLA models with world models. Existing coupling strategies fall into two suboptimal categories:

(a) Disentangled Interaction

World model acts as an external simulator. Structural isolation prevents the VLA from internalizing physical laws from WM latent states.

Examples: ADR1, ReSim

(b) Feature-Sharing

Shared representations, but no action-conditioned causal rollout. Planner cannot evaluate long-horizon action consequences.

Examples: HERMES-p, DriveVLA-W0

DriveWorld-VLA makes LLM hidden states the shared medium for both world modeling and action prediction, then adds an action-conditioned denoiser for counterfactual simulation within the same latent space (Figure 1, p. 1).

Method Overview

Multi-view images + BEV features + History actions + Text | [InternVL3-2B VLM] -> H_t (shared hidden state, B x L x D=1536) | | [CrossAttn BEV Tokenizer] | | +---> [Action Head (ACT)] -> A'_{t+dt} B_t' | | | [DENOISER Branch 1 | history-conditioned] | |-> B_{t+dt} | | | [DENOISER Branch 2 | action-conditioned DiT] | |-> B'_{t+dt} | | | [Reward R(B'_{t+dt}, B_{t+dt}, A'_{t+dt})] -> r_hat | [Reward-weighted action refinement: L'_act = r_hat * ||A' - A||^2 ]

Figure 2, p. 3. Three training stages build capability incrementally.

Three-Stage Progressive Training

1
VLA & WM Joint Training (Stage 1)

History-conditioned denoiser branch + action head trained jointly. Shared latent $H_t$ forces the backbone to encode physical dynamics. Loss: $\mathcal{L}_{s1} = \mathcal{L}_{seg} + \mathcal{L}_{act}$. (p. 4)

2
Action Controllability Fine-Tuning (Stage 2)

DiT flow-matching branch conditioned on GT future actions. Enables the model to map action sequences to corresponding future BEV latents. Loss: $\mathcal{L}_{s2} = \mathcal{L}_{FM}$. (p. 4-5)

3
Future-Guided Evaluation & Refinement (Stage 3)

Reward model scores each trajectory by comparing action-conditioned vs. history-conditioned BEV. Reward re-weights action loss. Denoiser and VLM frozen; only action head and reward model updated. Loss: $\mathcal{L}_{s3} = \mathcal{L}'_{act} + \mathcal{L}_{seg} + \mathcal{L}_{rew}$. (p. 5-6)

Key Formulas

Shared Latent + BEV Tokenization

$$H_t = \text{VLM}_\theta(I_t, B_t, A_{t-1}, T_t)$$ $$B_t' = \text{CrossAttn}_\theta(B_t, H_t, H_t)$$

Flow-Matching Denoiser Loss (Stage 2)

$$\mathcal{L}_{FM} = \left\|\text{DiT}_\theta\!\left(B_t', A_{t+\Delta t}, x^k, \tfrac{k}{N}\right) - \left(B_{t+\Delta t}' - x_0\right)\right\|^2$$

Reward-Weighted Action Loss (Stage 3)

$$\hat{r}_{t+\Delta t} = R(B_{t+\Delta t}', B_{t+\Delta t}, A_{t+\Delta t}')$$ $$\mathcal{L}'_{act} = \hat{r}_{t+\Delta t} \cdot \|A_{t+\Delta t}' - A_{t+\Delta t}\|^2$$

Equations (1-12), pp. 3-6. Euler sampling: $N=25$ steps. DiT: 12 blocks (Figure 3, p. 5).

Highlights

  • Stage 3 reward-weighted action loss (Eq. 11) directly grounds consequence-awareness in simulated BEV consistency without dense task labels.
  • Progressive training ablation (Table 5) quantifies -7.7 PDMS penalty for merging Stage 2+3, validating the sequential design.
  • Feature-level supervision ablation (Table 7) shows +3.4 PDMS gain, supporting the denoising loss as a load-bearing component.

Red Flags

Denoiser architecture underspecified: 12 DiT blocks (Figure 3) but hidden dimension, attention heads, and conditioning mechanism are not stated. Re-implementation is not straightforward.
Reward model training requires online simulation ("executing the predicted trajectories in a simulator," p. 6); the simulator identity and privilege level are undisclosed, making Stage 3 training not reproducible.
nuScenes L2 (0.61 m avg) is substantially higher than HERMES-p (0.36 m, Table 3). The paper does not investigate this trade-off; claiming SOTA on nuScenes via CR alone requires qualification.
NAVSIMv2 VLA-based comparison has only one baseline (DriveVLA-W0); other VLA methods present in NAVSIMv1 are absent without justification (Table 2, p. 5-6).

Fundamental Limitations

1

Reward model training depends on online simulation with undisclosed simulator access. Methods lacking this simulator cannot replicate Stage 3. (p. 6)

2

Reward operates in BEV latent space. Safety-critical events not captured by the BEV encoder are invisible to the reward signal, bounding its safety guarantees to BEV representation capacity.

3

Inference requires 25-step Euler sampling in Stage 3. Latency implications for real-time deployment are not discussed anywhere in the paper.

Implementation Details

NAVSIM Setup

  • Input: left-front + front + right-front stitched to 256x1024
  • BEV encoder: ResNet-34
  • Optimizer: AdamW, lr=1e-4, batch=16
  • 20 epochs/stage on 8 x NVIDIA H20
  • Total training: ~120 hours

nuScenes Setup

  • Input: 6-view cameras resized to 640x384
  • BEV encoder: Swin-T (pretrained)
  • Optimizer: AdamW, lr=7e-5, batch=1
  • 24 epochs/stage on 8 x NVIDIA H20
  • Total training: ~93 hours
  • Ego-state disabled for fair comparison

Section 4.1, p. 5. Vision-language representation: $H_t$ projected from $D=1536$ to $d=256$, then cross-attended to $N_L=700$ latent queries (Appendix A.3, p. 12).
