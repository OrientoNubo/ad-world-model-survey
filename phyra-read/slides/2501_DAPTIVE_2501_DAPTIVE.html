World Model Reinforcement Learning Autonomous Driving Finetuning

Problem: Performance Degradation during Finetuning

World model based RL uses a pretrain-finetune paradigm for autonomous driving. The pretrained dynamics model and policy are adapted to new tasks via online interaction. However, distribution shift between pretraining and new tasks causes performance degradation.

Dynamics Model Mismatch

The pretrained model fails to capture the new environment's state transition dynamics accurately.

Policy Mismatch

The pretrained policy selects suboptimal actions in the new task's state distribution.

Existing finetuning strategies (model-only, policy-only, alternating) do not identify which mismatch dominates, leading to inefficient or counterproductive updates (Figure 1, p.2).

Motivating Example

An agent pretrained on right turns at a four-way intersection is finetuned for left turns under similar traffic conditions. Three strategies are compared (Figure 1, p.2):

  • Model-only finetuning recovers fastest because the transition from right to left turns primarily challenges the dynamics model's spatial-temporal predictions.
  • Policy-only finetuning fails to address the dynamics model misalignment, resulting in decisions based on inaccurate predictions.
  • Alternate finetuning (Model+Policy) wastes computation updating the non-dominating component in each step.

This demonstrates that effective finetuning requires identifying the dominating mismatch at each step.

Theoretical Foundation: Theorem 1

The performance gap between pretraining and the new task is upper bounded by two separable terms (p.6):

$$\eta - \hat{\eta} \leq \underbrace{\left(\gamma^K \frac{\mathcal{E}_{\max}}{1-\gamma^K} + \Gamma \frac{2\gamma \mathcal{E}_P}{1-\gamma^K}\right)}_{\text{Dynamics Model Mismatch}} + \underbrace{\Gamma \left(\frac{4r_{\max}\mathcal{E}_\pi}{1-\gamma} + \frac{4\gamma\mathcal{E}_\pi}{1-\gamma^K}\right)}_{\text{Policy Mismatch}}$$

where $\mathcal{E}_P$ is the TV distance between true and predicted dynamics, $\mathcal{E}_\pi$ is the policy divergence, $K$ is the planning horizon, and $\gamma$ is the discount factor.

This decomposition yields a principled criterion: update the dynamics model when $\mathcal{E}_P \geq C_1 \mathcal{E}_\pi - C_2$; otherwise update the policy (p.6).

AdaWM: Method Overview

At each finetuning step, AdaWM executes two phases (Algorithm 1, p.7):

Phase 1: Mismatch Identification

Estimate TV distances for both dynamics model and policy using samples from replay buffer and current interactions.

$D_{TV}(P \| \hat{P})$ vs. $C \cdot D_{TV}(\pi_t \| \pi_\omega)$

Phase 2: Alignment-driven Finetuning

Selectively update the dominating component using efficient methods:

  • Model: LoRA-based low-rank update
  • Policy: Weighted ensemble of sub-units

Efficient Update Mechanisms

Dynamics Model (LoRA)

Parameters decomposed into base vector $Z$ and vector $\Phi$. Only the weight $B$ of the base vector is updated (p.7):

$B' \leftarrow B, \quad \phi' = (B'Z)^\top \Phi$

Based on NoLA (Koohpayegani et al., 2024)

Policy (Weighted Ensemble)

Policy network decomposed into $D$ sub-units. Only the weight vector $\Delta$ is updated (p.7):

$\Omega = \sum_{i=1}^{D} \delta_i \omega_i, \quad \Delta' \leftarrow \Delta$

Convex combination of sub-units

Paper Evaluation

Strengths

  • Principled theoretical decomposition of performance gap (Theorem 1) provides a clear criterion for adaptive updates
  • Effective motivating example builds strong intuition before formalization
  • Computationally efficient: LoRA for model, weighted ensemble for policy

Limitations

  • All experiments limited to CARLA simulator only (p.7); no real-world or cross-simulator validation
  • Parameter $C$ requires task-specific tuning despite theoretical derivation of $C_1, C_2$
  • Baselines (VAD, UniAD) are offline methods not designed for online finetuning

Key Takeaways

  1. Performance degradation in world model finetuning arises from two separable sources: dynamics model mismatch and policy mismatch. Blindly updating both or only one is suboptimal.
  2. AdaWM's mismatch identification via TV distance provides a computationally cheap diagnostic that guides selective updates at each finetuning step.
  3. The method achieves consistent gains across tasks of varying difficulty on CARLA, with SR improvements of 2x to 17x over supervised baselines (Table 2, p.8).

Open question: How well does the TV distance-based criterion transfer to continuous action spaces, higher-dimensional state representations, and real-world driving scenarios?
