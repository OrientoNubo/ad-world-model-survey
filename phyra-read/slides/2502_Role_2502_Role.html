Research Overview

This survey provides a systematic review of Driving World Models (DWMs), which predict future scene evolution from historical observations and actions. The paper categorizes DWMs by the modality of predicted scenes (2D image, 3D occupancy/point cloud, scene-free latent/behavioral) and reviews their applications in simulation, data generation, anticipative driving, and 4D pre-training.

The survey covers 50+ methods (Table 1, p.3), analyzes high-impact datasets (Table 2, p.6), and catalogues commonly used evaluation metrics (Table 3, p.7).

Claimed Contributions

  1. Comprehensive review of recent DWM progress, categorizing methods by predicted scene modality and summarizing their contributions to autonomous driving (p.1)
  2. Critical analysis of current limitations and promising future research directions to facilitate continued DWM evolution (p.1)
  3. Detailed categorization with a thorough summary of diverse DWM applications, differentiating this survey from concurrent works (p.1)

Architecture Trends

Based on Table 1 (p.3), two dominant architectures emerge across the 50+ surveyed methods:

  • Diffusion Models dominate 2D scene evolution, enabling high-fidelity, controllable video generation (DriveDreamer, Vista, Drive-WM, OccSora, HoloDrive)
  • Transformers are prevalent in 3D occupancy prediction (OccWorld, OccLLaMA) and latent state models (Think2Drive)

Emerging patterns include multi-modal large language models integrated with video diffusion (ADriver-I, OccLLaMA) and Gaussian Splatting for 3D scene representation (GaussianWorld).

Open Challenges and Future Directions (Sec. 5)

ChallengeDescription
Sparse DataCollecting driving data is costly, especially for rare/safety-critical scenarios like long-tail events. Limited and unevenly distributed datasets remain a bottleneck (p.7).
EfficiencyGenerative tasks increase computational cost and latency, hindering real-time applications. Detailed 4D generation exacerbates demands on computation and memory (p.7-8).
Reliable SimulationDWM performance degrades under complex conditions such as long-horizon rollouts, diverse weather/traffic, and view shifts (p.8).
Unified TaskCurrent DWMs predominantly predict scenes but lack implicit understanding of semantics (e.g., captioning, question answering) (p.8).
Multi-sensor ModelingSingle-modality sensor input is common; multi-sensor fusion for DWMs remains underexplored (p.8).
Adversarial RobustnessAdversarial attacks on DWMs can lead to severe safety incidents; defense strategies are critically needed (p.8).

Strengths: Broad coverage of 50+ methods with a clear taxonomy; useful Table 1 comparison; well-structured application categories; maintained GitHub repository as community resource.

Weaknesses: Per-method analysis is shallow due to scope; taxonomy by output modality alone misses other important axes (closed-loop capability, data efficiency); limited discussion of how DWMs compare against non-world-model baselines for the same tasks.

Key Takeaways

  • DWMs form a rapidly growing paradigm in autonomous driving, with diffusion models and transformers as the two primary architectural backbones.
  • The field is shifting from pure 2D image/video generation toward 3D (occupancy, point cloud) and scene-free (latent state) representations that are more directly useful for planning.
  • Four application pillars (simulation, data generation, anticipative driving, 4D pre-training) each derive distinct value from the world model paradigm.
  • Core bottlenecks remain in data scarcity for rare events, computational efficiency for real-time deployment, and adversarial robustness for safety-critical systems.
  • No unified evaluation benchmark exists across all DWM categories, making cross-method comparison difficult; this is a priority gap for the community.
