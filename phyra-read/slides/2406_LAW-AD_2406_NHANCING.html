LAW: Enhancing End-to-End Autonomous Driving with Latent World Model

Yingyan Li, Lue Fan, Jiawei He, Yuqi Wang, Yuntao Chen, Zhaoxiang Zhang, Tieniu Tan

CASIA / NLPR / MAIS / UCAS

ICLR 2025 | arXiv: 2406.08481

End-to-End Driving Latent World Model Self-Supervised Trajectory Prediction

Core Idea

LAW introduces a latent world model that predicts future scene features in latent space, conditioned on current visual latents and predicted ego trajectories. This creates a self-supervised training signal that jointly improves scene representation learning and trajectory prediction, without requiring any additional annotation.

Key insight: predicting future latent features (not images) is computationally efficient and forces the encoder to capture action-relevant dynamics.

Problem and Motivation

  • Underutilized sensor data: E2E planners process raw images but lack mechanisms to learn temporally rich features (p.1)
  • Slow generative world models: Image-based approaches (GAIA-1, Drive-WM) use diffusion, taking seconds per prediction (p.2)
  • Action-ignorant predictions: Existing temporal self-supervised methods ignore ego actions, despite ego motion being a major scene factor (p.1)

LAW addresses all three: it operates in latent space (fast), conditions on ego actions (action-aware), and provides a free self-supervised signal (no annotation).

Method: Pipeline Overview

Images(t) --> [Visual Encoder] --> V_t (visual latents) | V_t --> [Waypoint Decoder] --> W_t (predicted waypoints) | V_t + W_t --> [MLP] --> A_t (action-aware latents) | A_t --> [Latent World Model] --> V_hat(t+1) (predicted future latents) | MSE Loss <-- V(t+1) (encoder output on next frame)

The latent world model is used only during training. At inference, the encoder and decoder operate as usual.

Key Components

1. Action-aware Latents

Each visual latent $v_i$ is concatenated with flattened waypoints $\tilde{w}$ and projected through MLP:

$$a_i^t = \text{MLP}([v_i^t, \tilde{w}_i])$$

2. Latent World Model

Stacked transformer blocks with deformable self-attention predict future visual latents:

$$\hat{V}_{t+1} = \text{LatentWorldModel}(A_t)$$

3. Latent Supervision Loss

MSE between predicted and actual next-frame latents (from encoder):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{latent}} = \frac{1}{L}\sum_{i=1}^{L}\|\hat{v}_{t+1}^i - v_{t+1}^i\|_2$$

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Framework-agnostic: works with both perception-free and perception-based paradigms (Table 1, p.8)
  • Zero additional annotation required; only needs consecutive frames during training
  • Consistent SOTA across three diverse benchmarks covering open-loop and closed-loop evaluation

Weaknesses

  • MSE loss assumes deterministic future; cannot handle multi-modal driving scenarios (e.g., intersections)
  • World model used only during training; not leveraged at inference for planning or safety
  • No failure case analysis or latency measurements reported

Key Takeaways

  • Predicting future scene features in latent space (not pixel space) provides a computationally cheap yet effective self-supervised signal for E2E driving.
  • Action-conditioning is critical: the world model must know the ego trajectory to make useful predictions (Table 4 ablation).
  • The optimal prediction horizon is 1.5 seconds ahead; too short lacks information, too far introduces excessive uncertainty (Table 6).
  • A simple approach (transformer blocks + MSE loss) can achieve SOTA when applied to the right problem formulation.

Open direction: using the trained world model at inference for trajectory scoring and model-predictive-control style planning.
