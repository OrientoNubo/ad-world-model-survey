DrivingGen

A Comprehensive Benchmark for Generative Video World Models in Autonomous Driving
ICLR 2026 | Yang Zhou, Hao Shao, Letian Wang, Zhuofan Zong, Hongsheng Li, Steven L. Waslander
University of Toronto & CUHK MMLab
Benchmark World Models Autonomous Driving Video Generation

Problem & Motivation

Generative video world models are advancing rapidly for autonomous driving, yet the field lacks a benchmark tailored to driving-specific requirements. Existing evaluations suffer from three critical gaps:

Generic Metrics

FVD and human evaluation do not capture trajectory plausibility, agent consistency, or controllability (Table 1, p.2).

Dataset Bias

Over 80% of nuScenes and 90% of OpenDV data is sunny daytime driving in limited geographies (p.5, Figure 2a).

Missing Dimensions

No prior benchmark jointly evaluates distribution, quality, temporal consistency, and trajectory alignment (Table 1, p.2).

Hidden Failures

Single-metric evaluation masks critical issues: good FVD can coexist with stop-go jitter, identity drift, and non-physical agent disappearance (p.9).

Key Contributions

1. Diverse Evaluation Dataset

400 samples across two tracks (open-domain + ego-conditioned), with balanced weather (snow 13.1%, fog 12.6%), time-of-day, and global coverage across 7 world regions (p.3, Figure 2).

2. Multi-dimensional Metric Suite

Four evaluation dimensions: distribution (FVD, FTD), quality (visual + driving-specific + trajectory), temporal consistency (video, agent, trajectory), and trajectory alignment (ADE, DTW) (Table 2, p.6).

3. Comprehensive Model Evaluation

Benchmarks 14 models across 3 categories: general world models (7), physics-based models (2), and driving-specific models (5), revealing clear trade-offs between visual realism and physical plausibility (p.8-9).

Benchmark Architecture

DrivingGen takes an input scene (front-view RGB + scene description + optional ego trajectory) and passes generated videos through four evaluation dimensions:

DimensionMetricsWhat It Measures
DistributionFVD, FTDHow close generated videos/trajectories are to real data distributions
QualityCLIP-IQA+, MMP, Trajectory QualityPerceptual fidelity, driving-specific imaging, kinematic plausibility
Temporal ConsistencyVideo, Agent Appear., Agent Disapp., Traj. Consist.Frame-to-frame coherence at scene and agent level
Trajectory AlignmentADE, DTWAdherence to conditioned ego trajectory (ego-conditioned track only)

Novel Metric: Frechet Trajectory Distance (FTD)

Adapts the FID/FVD paradigm to trajectory space using a driving-domain encoder. Trajectories are sliced into windows of H=10 steps, encoded by MTR's agent_polyline_encoder into d-dimensional embeddings, then compared via Frechet distance (p.6, Appendix B.3).

FTD(X,Y) = ||mu_X - mu_Y||^2 + Tr(Sigma_X + Sigma_Y - 2*(Sigma_X^(1/2) * Sigma_Y * Sigma_X^(1/2))^(1/2))

Unlike FVD which only captures visual distribution, FTD directly measures whether generated motion patterns match real driving trajectory distributions.

Dataset Design

Open-Domain Track (200 samples)

  • Internet-sourced driving videos from worldwide cities
  • Tests generalization to unseen, diverse scenarios
  • Dense traffic, night driving, adverse weather, complex interactions

Ego-Conditioned Track (200 samples)

  • From 5 open-source datasets: Zod (Europe 26.5%), DrivingDojo (China 25.6%), CoVLA (Japan 27.4%), nuPlan (US 8.8%), WOMD (US 11.6%)
  • Tests trajectory controllability with given ego-trajectories

Diversity Balance

Weather: normal <60%, snow 13.1%, fog 12.6%, rain 22.5%. Time: day ~50%, night/sunset/sunrise ~50%. Regions: 7 global areas with no single region >27% (p.5-6, Figure 2).

Key Findings & Insights

Visual Quality vs. Physical Plausibility Trade-off

No single model excels in both. General world models produce visually appealing scenes but break physics; driving-specific models follow trajectories accurately but lag in image quality (p.8-9).

Closed-source Models Lead Overall

Kling 2.1 and Gen-3 consistently rank at top across both tracks, with strong visual quality, stable agent behavior, and rare abnormal disappearances (p.8).

Trajectory Alignment Remains Limited

Even under ego-trajectory conditioning, models show high ADE/DTW errors, indicating poor adherence to commanded paths due to both video artifacts affecting SLAM and imperfect motion generation (p.9).

Strengths & Weaknesses

Strengths

  • First to jointly evaluate 4 dimensions for driving world models
  • FTD brings FID paradigm to trajectory space with a domain-appropriate encoder
  • Robust SLAM pipeline with 100% reconstruction rate (Table 4, p.20)
  • Reveals hidden failure modes masked by single metrics

Weaknesses

  • Small dataset (400 samples) limits statistical power for close rankings
  • VLM-based agent disappearance detection is non-deterministic and opaque
  • No closed-loop evaluation; open-loop only
  • SLAM quality coupled with video quality conflates visual and trajectory errors

Limitations & Open Directions

  • Dataset scale: 400 samples constrain statistical confidence; scaling to 2000+ with stratified sampling is directly feasible (p.10)
  • Closed-loop evaluation: Integration with CARLA/Navsim would test whether generated futures enable safe planning (p.10)
  • Multi-modal data: Extending to LiDAR, HD maps, and multi-view would better serve the full AD stack (p.10)
  • Scene controllability: Evaluating whether models can follow scene-level instructions (object placement, layout control) remains unaddressed (p.10)
  • Counterfactual reasoning: Testing with hypothetical or modified scenarios would probe model understanding beyond memorization (p.10)
