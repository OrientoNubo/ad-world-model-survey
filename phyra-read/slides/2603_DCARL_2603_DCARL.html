DCARL

A Divide-and-Conquer Framework for Autoregressive Long-Trajectory Video Generation
Junyi Ouyang, Wenbin Teng, Gonglin Chen, Yajie Zhao, Haiwei Chen  |  USC ICT  |  arXiv 2603.24835  |  2026-03-25
Long-Trajectory Video Autoregressive Diffusion Divide-and-Conquer Camera Control AD World Modeling

Abstract

Long-trajectory video generation is a crucial yet challenging task for world modeling, primarily due to the limited scalability of existing video diffusion models (VDMs). Autoregressive models, while offering infinite rollout, suffer from visual drift and poor controllability. To address these issues, we propose DCARL, a novel divide-and-conquer, autoregressive framework that effectively combines the structural stability of the divide-and-conquer scheme with the high-fidelity generation of VDMs. Our approach first employs a dedicated Keyframe Generator trained without temporal compression to establish long-range, globally consistent structural anchors. Subsequently, an Interpolation Generator synthesizes the dense frames in an autoregressive manner with overlapping segments, utilizing the keyframes for global context and a single clean preceding frame for local coherence. Trained on a large-scale internet long trajectory video dataset, our method achieves superior performance in both visual quality (lower FID and FVD) and camera adherence (lower ATE and ARE) compared to state-of-the-art autoregressive and divide-and-conquer baselines, demonstrating stable and high-fidelity generation for long trajectory videos up to 32 seconds in length.

Problem and Motivation

Core Problem

Pure autoregressive video diffusion accumulates exposure bias at O(N) or O(L^N) rate as trajectory length grows. Attention sink stabilization partially helps but causes sink collapse that degrades camera responsiveness. No existing method explicitly bounds error propagation to a finite window.

Core Argument

Joint, non-autoregressive keyframe synthesis converts an unbounded AR error problem into a bounded local interpolation problem where each frame's error is a convex combination of its two anchor keyframe errors plus a local variance term independent of N (Proposition 1, Appendix A).

Key Design Choices

Motion-Inductive Noisy Conditioning

Perturbs keyframe latents during both training and inference: z̃_Ki = α_c·z_Ki + σ_c·ε (α_c=0.7, σ_c=0.3). Prevents identity-mapping shortcut where model copies keyframe pixels.

Seamless Boundary Consistency

Replaces first p latents of each segment with clean preceding-segment latents at every denoising step. Trained with this substitution to eliminate distribution mismatch at junctions.

Phyra's Judgment

Highlights

  • Motion-Inductive Noisy Conditioning reduces FVD 246.5 → 201.7 (Tab. 5) by forcing GI to learn temporal dynamics rather than copy keyframe pixels.
  • Image-level VAE encoding reduces keyframe FID 19.6 → 16.3 and ATE 0.155 → 0.100 (Tab. 3) by eliminating lossy temporal downsampling on sparse frames.
  • Proposition 1 (Brownian Bridge formalization) provides a falsifiable bound: interpolation error is a convex combination of anchor errors plus a local variance term independent of sequence length N.
  • Zero-shot nuScenes FVD 225.4 vs. SEVA 487.9 demonstrates out-of-distribution temporal stability without domain-specific fine-tuning.

Weaknesses

  • Keyframe Generator quality ceiling is not analyzed independently; total video quality is bounded below by GK errors that GI cannot correct.
  • Optimal inference stride ∆ktest = 8 is not derived by any principled criterion and may require re-tuning for new domains (Tab. 4 shows ATE varies 0.096–0.148 across stride choices).
  • Baseline comparison excludes Epona, LIVE, and other recent divide-and-conquer methods cited in Related Work; "state-of-the-art" claim is not fully supported.
  • No latency or memory cost comparison is provided despite two-model architecture being inherently more expensive than single-model AR baselines.

Fundamental Limitations

  • Two-model architecture doubles training and inference compute; joint keyframe synthesis does not scale to very long videos (e.g., 2+ minutes) without a hierarchical extension of GK.
  • Any keyframe quality failure propagates irreversibly to the dense sequence because GI is conditioned on GK outputs without a feedback correction loop.
  • The Lipschitz assumption L ≥ 1 in Proposition 1 is not empirically verified on the trained model; the formal bound may not be tight in practice.

Open Questions

  • How does keyframe quality degrade as total video length increases beyond 32 seconds, and is there an upper bound on M for joint synthesis?
  • Do optimal hyperparameters (α_c, σ_c, p, ∆ktest) transfer from 16-second ablation conditions to 32-second or longer sequences?
  • What is the wall-clock latency and GPU memory footprint of DCARL (both stages combined) vs. SEVA and DiffF at 32-second generation?
