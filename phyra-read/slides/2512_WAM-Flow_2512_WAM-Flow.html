Research Overview

WAM-Flow formulates ego-trajectory planning as discrete flow matching over a structured token space, enabling fully parallel bidirectional denoising rather than sequential autoregressive decoding. Built on a converted Janus-1.5B backbone, it introduces a metric-aligned numerical tokenizer to preserve scalar geometry, a geometry-aware flow objective, and simulator-guided GRPO to enforce safety and comfort alignment. The model achieves 90.3 PDMS on NAVSIM-v1 with 5-step inference and 89.1 PDMS with 1-step at 0.09s latency (Table 6, p.8).

Problem and Motivation

The Three Obstacles for Discrete Flow Matching in VLA

ObstacleSpecific DescriptionWAM-Flow Solution
Training data/compute costDFM models cannot be trained from scratch efficiently; generic VLM backbones lack road-scene competence (p.3)Multi-stage adaptation: domain VQA pretraining on 6.5M data (+3.3 PDMS, Fig. 6)
Text token metric blindnessStandard vocabulary embeddings do not preserve distance/ordering among scalar trajectory values (p.3-4)Metric-aligned numerical tokenizer with triplet-margin ranking loss (+4.9+2.3 PDMS, Table 5)
Likelihood-only trainingSupervised flow matching does not enforce safety, ego-progress, comfort in closed-loop (p.5)Simulator-guided GRPO with composite safety-performance reward (+3.4 PDMS, Table 5)

The authors argue that prior VLA planners fail to combine efficiency, accuracy, and safety because they face both a token representation gap (text tokens cannot encode metric relationships) and a training objective gap (likelihood maximization does not align with closed-loop constraints). Addressing both simultaneously within the discrete flow matching framework enables coarse-to-fine trajectory planning with a tunable compute-accuracy trade-off.

Key Module: Metric-Aligned Numerical Tokenizer

Standard text token embeddings do not preserve metric structure among scalar values. WAM-Flow introduces a codebook of N = 20,001 tokens covering [-100, 100] with 0.01 resolution. Each scalar v is projected to a d-dimensional embedding and L2-normalized: $z = E(v)/\|E(v)\|_2$. A triplet-margin ranking loss enforces that embedding distances reflect scalar proximity.

$$\mathcal{L}_{num} = \mathbb{E}_{(i,j,k)\sim\mathcal{T}}\left[\max\left(0,\, d_{ij} - d_{ik} + \alpha\right)\right]$$

where $|v_i - v_j| < |v_i - v_k|$   Triplet margin $\alpha = 0.05$   (p.4, Eq. 5)

Ablation (Table 5, p.7): numerical tokenizer +4.9 PDMS; metric-aligned embeddings +2.3 PDMS, totaling +7.2 PDMS over standard Janus text tokenizer.

Key Module: Geometry-Aware Discrete Flow Matching

The probability path is a Gibbs distribution induced by the tokenizer-specific metric $d(x, x_1)$, with inverse temperature $\beta_t$ increasing from 0 to $\infty$:

$$p_t(x|x_1) = \text{softmax}\!\left(-\beta_t\, d(x, x_1)\right), \quad \beta_0 = 0,\; \beta_1 \to \infty$$

The conditional CTMC rate steers transitions toward the target by assigning higher rates to states closer to $x_1$:

$$u_t(x, z \mid x_1) = p_t(x|x_1)\,\dot{\beta}_t\,\bigl[d(z, x_1) - d(x, x_1)\bigr]_+$$

Inference timestep schedule: $\beta_t = 3 \times \left(\frac{t}{1-t}\right)^{0.9}$   (p.6). Parallel decoding over all trajectory tokens enables the coarse-to-fine speed-accuracy trade-off.

Key Module: Simulator-Guided GRPO

The composite NAVSIM reward decomposes into a multiplicative safety penalty and a weighted performance average:

$$R(\tau) = \prod_{m\in\{NC,DAC\}} s_m(\tau) \cdot \frac{\sum_{w\in\{EP,TTC,C\}}\lambda_w s_w(\tau)}{\sum_{w}\lambda_w}$$

Default weights EP:TTC:Comfort = 5:5:2   (Table 3, p.8)

G = 3 candidate trajectories per context (Table 2, p.7). Group baseline reduces variance; KL term anchors updates to supervised reference. GRPO trained 0.5 epoch on 103K NAVSIM scenes, gaining +3.4 PDMS over SFT-only (Table 5, p.7).

Phyra Highlights

  • The metric-aligned tokenizer directly addresses a known deficiency of text-token-based trajectory prediction; the +4.9 PDMS ablation (Table 5) is a concrete, isolated measurement of this component's contribution (p.6-7)
  • The coarse-to-fine property is quantified with explicit inference times: 1-step at 0.09s achieves 89.1 PDMS, 5-step at 0.48s achieves 90.3, giving users a practical speed-accuracy knob (Table 6, p.8)
  • Simulator-guided GRPO adds +3.4 PDMS over the SFT baseline when combined with pretraining (Table 5), demonstrating that closed-loop reward alignment provides gains that supervised training alone cannot capture

Phyra Weaknesses

  • The GRPO reward directly encodes NAVSIM PDMS sub-metrics, creating a structural benchmark-overfitting concern; generalization to other closed-loop simulators is unverified
  • Only single front-view camera is used, making comparisons with multi-camera/LiDAR baselines in Table 1 partially a sensor-modality comparison rather than a paradigm comparison
  • No open-loop L2/FDE metrics are reported; all evaluation is closed-loop NAVSIM, preventing comparison with open-loop literature
  • The speedup claim (4.67x over ReCogDrive) conflates paradigm advantage with backbone-size advantage (1.5B vs. 8B)

Fundamental Limitations

Structural

  • GRPO reward is derived from the same NAVSIM metrics used for evaluation, creating test-set leakage that cannot be eliminated without a held-out simulator
  • Fixed N=20,001 codebook over [-100, 100] cannot represent trajectories outside this range or requiring sub-centimeter precision

Experimental

  • 4-stage curriculum on 4x8 Ascend 910B NPUs limits reproducibility for most researchers
  • Single-camera constraint prevents fair comparison with multi-view baselines; effect on safety in lateral scenarios is unknown

Open Questions and Improvement Directions

Outstanding Questions

  • Does the metric-aligned tokenizer improve coarse-to-fine refinement curves specifically, or does the PDMS gain come primarily from better SFT initialization?
  • What is the GRPO policy's behavior on out-of-distribution scenarios not covered by NAVSIM test scenes?
  • How does performance degrade when the front-view camera faces adverse illumination, given the single-camera constraint?
  • Would a data-driven codebook (k-means on trajectory statistics) improve quantization efficiency over the uniform [-100, 100] grid?

Improvement Directions

  • Controlled sensor parity: Extend to multi-view input (3-6 cameras) to separate DFM paradigm contribution from sensor-modality difference. Feasibility: moderate.
  • Adaptive step selection: Use posterior distribution confidence to trigger early stopping for simple scenes, avoiding the fixed n-step overhead. Feasibility: high; consistent with coarse-to-fine design.
  • Cross-simulator GRPO evaluation: Evaluate zero-shot on nuPlan reactive simulation after NAVSIM-GRPO training to test safety alignment generalization. Feasibility: moderate.
